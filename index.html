Component 1 · Religion 1
Christianity & Catholic Christianity
Beliefs, Teachings and Practices
Introduction

Introduction to Christianity

Christianity is based on the life and teachings of Jesus Christ. The sacred text is the Bible, split into the Old Testament (at least 39 books, shared with Jewish scripture) and the New Testament (27 books including the four Gospels).

The different branches of Christianity are called denominations — they share key beliefs but worship differently.
Roman Catholics follow the Pope. Protestants include Anglicans, Baptists and Methodists. Orthodox Christians follow the eastern traditions.
The Nicene Creed (agreed at the Council of Nicaea, 325 AD) is the key statement of shared Christian belief.
Core Belief

The Trinity

Although Christians believe in one God, they believe God exists in three 'persons': the Father, the Son (Jesus) and the Holy Spirit.

At Jesus's baptism (Matthew 3:16–17), all three parts of the Trinity are present — the Spirit descends, God speaks from heaven, and Jesus is in the river.
St Paul described Jesus as having "equality with God" (Philippians 2:6).
The Council of Constantinople (381 AD) further defined the nature of the Trinity in the Nicene Creed.
"This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased."
Matthew 3:17 NIV
Origins

Creation

God created the world in six days according to Genesis 1. Christians see God the Father as creator, with the Spirit and the Son also involved.

Day 1: light & darkness. Day 2: sky. Day 3: oceans, land, plants. Day 4: sun, moon, stars. Day 5: sea creatures & birds. Day 6: land animals & people.
God created humans in his image (imago dei) — unique among all creation.
Some Christians take Genesis literally (Creationism); others see it as symbolic and compatible with science (theistic evolution).
"In the beginning was the Word... and the Word was God."
John 1:1 NIV
Problem of Evil

Evil and Suffering

The Fall: Evil entered the world when Adam and Eve disobeyed God in the Garden of Eden. Every human is now born with original sin — a flawed nature.
Free will: God gave humans the freedom to choose good or evil — evil is the result of bad choices.
Moral evil: caused by human actions (e.g. war, murder). Natural evil: natural disasters not caused by humans.
The theodicy problem: how can a good, all-powerful God allow suffering? Christians answer this in different ways.
Afterlife

The Afterlife

Christianity teaches that the soul lives on after death (immortality of the soul) and the body will be resurrected for Judgement Day.

Heaven: a place of great beauty and eternal closeness with God, for those who are judged well.
Hell: eternal separation from God, for those who rejected him.
Purgatory (Catholic): a state where souls are purified before entering heaven.
"I am the resurrection and the life. The one who believes in me will live, even though they die."
John 11:25 NIV
Jesus

Jesus Christ & Salvation

Christians believe Jesus is the Son of God who became human — the Incarnation.

An angel told Mary she would give birth to the Son of God (Luke 1:35).
Crucifixion: Jesus was crucified to atone for human sins — this is the Atonement.
Resurrection: Jesus rose from the dead on the third day, giving Christians hope of life after death.
Ascension: Jesus returned to heaven 40 days after the resurrection (Luke 24:51).
Salvation: through faith in Jesus, Christians believe they can be saved and enter heaven.
Worship

Different Forms of Worship

Worship is a Christian's way of expressing love and devotion to God. Sunday is the 'Lord's Day' and most churches hold their main service on Sunday morning.

Anglican services usually include the Eucharist (also called Holy Communion or the Lord's Supper).
Catholic services (Mass) always include the Eucharist and include more formal rituals.
Non-conformist services (Baptists, Methodists) focus more on preaching and Bible study.
Charismatic worship is more informal, with songs, clapping and speaking in tongues.
Quakers worship in silence, waiting for the Holy Spirit to inspire them to speak.
Sacraments

The Sacraments

A Sacrament is a ceremony through which Christians believe they receive God's grace.

Roman Catholics & Orthodox recognise seven sacraments: Baptism, Confirmation, Eucharist, Penance, Anointing of the Sick, Holy Orders, and Matrimony.
Most Protestants accept only Baptism and the Eucharist as sacraments — they believe only these were prescribed by Jesus.
Quakers & the Salvation Army don't celebrate any sacraments, seeing them as unnecessary symbols.
Baptism marks official entry into the Church. Confirmation (Catholic) is when someone renews their baptismal vows.
"The seven sacraments touch all the stages and all the important moments of Christian life..."
Catechism of the Catholic Church, 1210
Devotion

Prayer & Pilgrimage

Prayer: communicating with God. Believers draw comfort knowing God is listening. Most denominations have formal, set prayers.
The Lord's Prayer is the most important. It was taught by Jesus and covers key themes including the kingdom of God, forgiveness and temptation.
Informal prayer is spontaneous and personal. Some Christians pray in tongues (glossolalia).
Pilgrimage: journeys to sacred sites help deepen faith. Key sites include Lourdes (France), Jerusalem (Israel), and Rome (Italy).
Festivals

Christmas & Easter

Christmas (25 Dec): celebrates Jesus's birth in Bethlehem. Preceded by Advent (4 Sundays of preparation). Orthodox Christians celebrate on 7 January.
Easter: the most important festival. Good Friday marks the crucifixion; Easter Sunday celebrates the resurrection.
Lent: 40 days of prayer and fasting before Easter, recalling Jesus's 40 days in the desert.
Pentecost: celebrates the descent of the Holy Spirit on the disciples 50 days after Easter.
Community

The Work of the Church

Churches play an important role in local communities — food banks, support groups, youth clubs and pastoral care.
Christians believe in justice — all people are equal in God's eyes (Matthew 25:40).
Reconciliation: the Corrymeela Community (Northern Ireland) and Pax Christi (Catholic) work to heal conflict and promote peace.
Christian Aid, CAFOD and Tearfund work globally to relieve poverty and respond to disasters.

Component 1 · Religion 2
Islam
Beliefs, Teachings and Practices
Introduction

Introduction to Islam

Islam was founded in the 7th century. Muslims believe in one god, Allah. The Qur'an is the holy book — Muslims also follow the Prophet Muhammad's teachings (the Sunnah and Hadith).

About 85–90% of Muslims are Sunni. Most of the rest are Shi'a. The split arose over who should lead after Muhammad's death.
Sunnis accepted Abu Bakr as the first caliph. Shi'as believed Ali (Muhammad's cousin and son-in-law) should have been first.
Muhammad received the Qur'an as a revelation from Allah through the angel Jibril (Gabriel).
Core Beliefs

Key Beliefs in Islam

Islam is a monotheistic religion — Muslims believe in only one god, Allah.

Tawhid: the oneness of Allah — the most fundamental belief. Allah has 99 names (e.g. al-Ahad = the one and only; al-Rahman = the compassionate).
Shirk (associating partners with Allah) is the greatest sin. This is why images of Muhammad are forbidden.
Angels (Malaikah): serve Allah and carry out his will. Key angels: Jibril (revelation), Mika'il (nature), Israfil (Last Day), Azra'il (death).
Prophets (Nubuwwah): Allah sent many prophets to guide humanity — including Ibrahim, Musa, Dawud, Isa (Jesus) and Muhammad, the final prophet.
Holy Books: the Suhuf Ibrahim (lost), Tawrat (Torah), Zabur (Psalms), Injil (Gospel), and the Qur'an — the only one that exists in its original form.
"Say, 'He is Allah, [who is] One!'"
Qur'an 112:1
Akhirah

Life After Death

Al-Qadr (predestination): Allah has decided everything that will happen. Muslims believe in a mix of predestination and free will.
Yawm ad-Din (Day of Judgement): all people will be judged by Allah on the basis of their deeds. Their actions are recorded by recording angels.
Jannah: paradise, described as a garden of peace and pleasure. Jahannam: a place of fire and suffering.
Sunnis tend to believe Allah knows everything predestined; Shi'as place slightly more emphasis on free will.
Practice

The Five Pillars of Islam (Sunni)

Five key actions all Sunni Muslims must try to follow:

1. Shahadah: Declaration of faith — "There is no god but Allah, and Muhammad is his messenger." Said daily and at key moments of life.
2. Salah: Prayer five times a day (Fajr, Zuhr, Asr, Maghrib, Isha). The rak'ah is the set sequence of prayer positions.
3. Zakah: Giving 2.5% of savings to those in need each year. Purifies wealth and helps the community.
4. Sawm: Fasting during Ramadan (the 9th month). Muslims fast from dawn to sunset. Ends with Eid ul-Fitr.
5. Hajj: Pilgrimage to Makkah at least once in a lifetime (if able). ~2 million Muslims go each year during Dhu'l-Hijja.
Shi'a Practice

Ten Obligatory Acts (Shi'a)

The Ten Obligatory Acts for Shi'as include Salah, Sawm, Zakah and Hajj, plus additional duties like Khums (20% tax on surplus income) and Jihad.
Shi'a prayers differ slightly: worshippers touch their forehead to a clay or wooden tablet (often from Karbala) during prostration.
Imamah: Belief in the Imams (descendants of Ali) as spiritual and political leaders is a key Shi'a belief, not shared by Sunnis.
Holy Sites

Hajj — The Pilgrimage

All pilgrims wear simple ihram (white clothing) to show equality before Allah.
The Ka'aba: a giant stone cube in Makkah — the holiest place in Islam. Pilgrims circle it seven times anticlockwise.
Sa'y: walking between the hills of Safa and Marwa, recalling Hajar's search for water.
Arafat: pilgrims stand for hours in prayer on the Plain of Arafat — the spiritual peak of Hajj.
Stoning the devil: throwing pebbles at three pillars in Mina, symbolising Ibrahim's rejection of Shaytan.
Celebrations

Islamic Festivals

Eid ul-Fitr: 'Festival of the Breaking of the Fast' — marks the end of Ramadan. Muslims pray, give Zakah al-Fitr, and celebrate with family.
Eid ul-Adha: 'Festival of Sacrifice' — commemorates Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice Isma'il. An animal is sacrificed and divided three ways (family, relatives, charity).
Both Eids are important to Sunnis and Shi'as. Shi'as also observe Ashura, marking the martyrdom of Husayn at Karbala.
"My son, indeed I have seen in a dream that I [must] sacrifice you..."
Qur'an 37:102

Thematic Study · Theme A
Relationships & Families
All religions — religious and non-religious views in Britain
Sexuality

Sexuality & Sexual Relationships

Traditionally, all three religions teach that sex should only occur within marriage — sex outside of marriage is seen as a sin.
Christianity: The Catechism of the Catholic Church says "Sexuality is ordered to the conjugal love of man and woman" (2360). The Church of England has had debates on same-sex relationships.
Islam: Qur'an 23:7 states that believers abstain from sexual relations except with their spouses.
Homosexuality: traditional religious views are that homosexual sex is wrong; many modern denominations have become more accepting.
Non-religious view: consensual sex between adults is a matter of personal choice.
Family Planning

Contraception

Contraception (birth control) can be temporary (pill, condoms) or permanent (sterilisation).
Atheists & humanists generally have no objection — they think it's better to only have children when wanted.
Roman Catholics oppose all artificial contraception, believing it interferes with God's plan for sexuality (Humanae Vitae, 1968). Natural family planning is permitted.
Most Protestants accept contraception within marriage.
Islam: some forms are permitted (those that prevent fertilisation), but not those that destroy a fertilised egg.
Marriage

Marriage & Divorce

Marriage rates in the UK have been declining; same-sex marriage is now legal in England, Scotland and Wales.
Christianity: Marriage is a covenant with God, not just a contract. Roman Catholics believe it is indissoluble — divorce is not recognised by the Church (annulment is possible).
Islam: Marriage is Allah's will (Qur'an 24:32; 30:21). Nikah is the Islamic marriage contract. Divorce (talaq) is permitted but strongly discouraged — "the most detestable of lawful things" (Hadith).
Non-religious view: marriage is a personal choice; cohabitation is increasingly common and acceptable.
Family Life

Families

A stable family gives children identity, security, and teaches right from wrong.
The nuclear family (married man and woman + children) was long seen as the ideal in Britain. Religious families are more likely to follow this model.
Today the term 'family' includes same-sex parents, single parents, and reconstituted families.
For religious people, family life is key to passing on faith to the next generation.
Islam: family is central; parents are deeply respected (Qur'an 17:23 commands kindness to parents).
Equality

Gender Equality

Gender discrimination is gradually improving in the UK. Fixed ideas about gender roles are giving way to greater equality.
Christianity: Galatians 3:28 says there is "neither male nor female" in Christ. But 1 Timothy 2:12 has historically been used to limit women's roles. Women can now be Anglican bishops but not Roman Catholic or Orthodox priests.
Islam: men and women are equal in religious obligation. Qur'an 33:35 lists equal duties. Women lead prayers in private settings; in mosques, they pray separately.
The Equality Act 2010 makes discrimination on the grounds of sex illegal in the UK.
B
Thematic Study · Theme B
Religion & Life
Origins, environment, animal rights, life & death
Origins

The Origins of the Universe

The Big Bang theory: the Universe began in a massive explosion of matter and energy ~13.8 billion years ago. The Universe is still expanding.
Evolution (Darwin): life on Earth evolved from simple cells over millions of years — survival of the fittest.
Religious views: many believers see the Big Bang and evolution as compatible with God creating through these processes (theistic evolution). Some take Genesis literally (creationism).
Islam: The Qur'an says Allah created the heavens and earth in "six days" (Qur'an 7:54). Some interpret this symbolically.
Environment

The Environment & Stewardship

Global warming: greenhouse gases trap heat, causing temperatures to rise, ice to melt and sea levels to rise.
Dominion (Genesis 1:26): God gave humans power over creation. Some interpret this as a licence to use the environment; most Christians reject this.
Stewardship: God made humans caretakers of creation — we have a duty to protect it. This is the mainstream Christian and Islamic view.
Islam: humans are khalifah (stewards/vicegerents) of Allah on Earth and will be judged on how they cared for creation.
"...cannot be understood as licence to abuse, spoil, squander or destroy what God has made."
Christian Declaration on Nature, Assisi 1986
Ethics

Animal Rights

Animal testing: used to test medical and cosmetic products. Argued for using utilitarianism (the greater good); many religious people oppose unnecessary animal suffering.
Catholic Church: tolerates animal testing if it reduces significant human suffering and is within reasonable limits.
Christianity: only humans are created in God's image (imago dei) — human life is sacred above animal life. But animals are part of God's creation and should be treated with care.
Islam: Muslims must avoid cruelty to animals (the Prophet forbade it). Halal slaughter aims to minimise suffering.
Sanctity of Life

Abortion & Euthanasia

Abortion: legal in England, Scotland and Wales up to 24 weeks. After 24 weeks only in specific circumstances.
Christianity: life is sacred. Many oppose abortion. Some accept it in exceptional cases (e.g. rape, risk to mother's life). The Catholic Church opposes it absolutely.
Islam: life is sacred (Qur'an 5:32). Abortion is generally wrong but permitted if the mother's life is at risk. Ensoulment is believed to occur at 120 days.
Active euthanasia: illegal in the UK, but legal in some countries (e.g. Belgium, Netherlands). Most religious groups oppose it.
Passive euthanasia: withdrawing treatment is more widely accepted. Anglican Church says death may be a blessing rather than extending life artificially.
Death

The Afterlife

Christianity: heaven or hell based on God's judgement. Catholics also believe in purgatory.
Islam: after death, the soul enters Barzakh (a waiting state) until the Day of Judgement. Then Jannah or Jahannam.
Non-religious views: humanists believe death is simply the end of existence, making this life all the more precious.
Belief in the afterlife influences how people live — trying to live according to God's will to receive a reward.

Thematic Study · Theme C
The Existence of God & Revelation
Arguments for and against the existence of God
Arguments For

Design & Causation Arguments

The Design Argument (Teleological): the world appears designed (e.g. human eyes, ecosystems) so there must be a designer — God. Proposed by William Paley: the watchmaker analogy.
The First Cause Argument (Cosmological): everything is caused by something else. There must be an uncaused first cause — God. Associated with Thomas Aquinas.
The Muslim theologian al-Ghazali further developed the cosmological argument in his book al-Iqtisad fil'Itiqad.
Counter-arguments: the universe could be eternal (no need for a first cause); the universe may have arisen by chance; an imperfect world suggests a flawed or absent designer.
Evidence

Miracles

Miracles are seemingly inexplicable events — e.g. healings, appearances, natural phenomena.
Christianity: Jesus performed miracles to show God's power — feeding the 5000, healing the blind, raising Lazarus.
Islam: the greatest miracle is the Qur'an itself — its literary perfection is seen as proof of divine origin. Muhammad also performed miracles (e.g. Isra and Mi'raj).
Counter-argument: miracles may have natural explanations; they happened before science could explain them; faith doesn't require miracles.
Revelation

Revelation

Special Revelation: to specific people — e.g. Allah's revelation of the Qur'an to Muhammad; God speaking to Moses from the burning bush; the Incarnation.
General Revelation: available to everyone — through the natural world (numinous experiences) and reason.
Numinous experiences: awe-inspiring moments that suggest a divine presence — e.g. a beautiful sunset, a vast ocean.
Visions: Abraham received God's word in a vision (Genesis 15:4); Jacob saw angels on a ladder to heaven (Genesis 28); Moses saw the burning bush (Exodus 3).
Scripture: all three Abrahamic religions point to sacred texts as a form of revelation from God.
Arguments Against

Arguments Against God's Existence

Lack of evidence: atheists and humanists say there is no scientific proof of God's existence.
Science explains creation: the Big Bang theory and evolution explain the origin of the universe and life without needing a God.
Problem of evil: if God is omnipotent and omnibenevolent, why does suffering exist? This challenges belief in a benevolent creator.
Religious experiences: may be explained by psychology, brain activity, or wishful thinking — not divine intervention.
Inconsistencies in religion: different religions have different Gods — they can't all be right.
Positions

Types of Belief

Theist: believes in God.
Atheist: does not believe in God — no convincing evidence exists.
Agnostic: believes it is impossible to know for certain whether God exists.
Humanist: focuses on human reason and ethics without religion; generally atheist.
About 84% of the global population belongs to a religion (Pew Research Center).
D
Thematic Study · Theme D
Religion, Peace & Conflict
War, terrorism, justice, forgiveness and reconciliation
Peace

Peace & Conflict

Peace means everyone in the world living in harmony with no conflict. The UN works towards this goal.
Christianity, Islam and (in other sections) other religions all encourage believers to work toward peace.
Pacifism: the belief that war and violence are always wrong. Pacifists believe all disputes should be settled peacefully. Some Christians refused to fight in World Wars as "conscientious objectors".
Just War Theory: a war can be justified if: there is a good reason (e.g. self-defence), all peaceful options have been tried, it is started by a proper authority, it has a chance of success, and the harm caused is less than the harm being prevented.
Christianity

Christianity & Peace

Jesus taught "Blessed are the peacemakers" (Matthew 5:9). Christians believe violence goes against Jesus's teachings.
Dorothy Day: Catholic activist who followed Jesus's pacifist teachings; protested against war and co-founded "The Catholic Worker" newspaper.
Archbishop Oscar Romero: worked for peace in 1970s El Salvador, raised awareness of human rights abuses, and was killed for his beliefs in 1980.
Some Christians accept Just War and self-defence; others, like Quakers, are absolute pacifists.
Islam

Islam & Peace

Islam promotes living a peaceful existence — the Qur'an teaches that people should be kind even if not treated well.
Greater Jihad: the personal, internal struggle to live according to Allah's teachings.
Lesser Jihad: the external struggle to defend Islam — can be peaceful (campaigning) or, in limited circumstances, military (just war conditions).
Muslims believe Allah will judge all actions. Fighting is only permissible in self-defence or against oppression.
"And the servants of the Most Merciful are those who walk upon the earth easily, and when the ignorant address them [harshly], they say [words of] peace."
Qur'an 25:63
WMDs

Weapons of Mass Destruction

WMDs (chemical, biological, nuclear) are indiscriminate — they harm civilians and soldiers alike. Chemical and biological weapons are banned by international law.
Arguments for nuclear weapons: deterrent effect (MAD — Mutually Assured Destruction), protect national security.
Arguments against: indiscriminate, disproportionate, violates sanctity of life, enormous cost better spent on the poor, nuclear winter risk.
Most religious groups oppose WMDs as fundamentally contrary to just war principles.
Resolution

Peacemaking — Justice & Forgiveness

Justice: God treats and judges people fairly — believers should strive for fairness and equality in society.
Forgiveness: God is merciful, and believers should reflect this by forgiving others. It allows both victims and offenders to move on.
Reconciliation: bringing conflicting parties together to make peace. Christian Aid has urged compromise in the Israel/Palestine conflict.
Religious organisations offer relief to war-torn areas, campaign for human rights, and work to end conflicts through dialogue.
E
Thematic Study · Theme E
Religion, Crime & Punishment
Law, justice, forgiveness and capital punishment
Law

Religion & the Law

Most religions teach that people should follow the law, as it protects people and reflects God's will.
Christianity: the 'sheep and goats' parable (Matthew 25:31–46) teaches that everyone will be judged by God. Helping others = serving Jesus.
Islam: on the Day of Judgement, those who've committed crimes may not be sent to Jannah. Murder is especially condemned (Qur'an 5:32 — killing one person is like killing all of humanity).
Hate crime: any crime committed because of ethnicity, religion etc. Christianity, Islam and (in other sections) Judaism all condemn hatred of other groups.
Murder and theft are condemned by all three Abrahamic religions as they break the Ten Commandments and religious moral law.
Forgiveness

Forgiveness

God is merciful and forgiving — believers should reflect this in their treatment of criminals.
Forgiving criminals allows them to be reconciled with society, reducing reoffending. If released with no prospects, crime may seem the only option.
Restorative justice: brings victims and offenders together to resolve conflict and facilitate rehabilitation.
Most religious people believe criminals should still face punishment — forgiveness doesn't mean there are no consequences.
Punishment

Purposes of Punishment

Retribution: 'paying back' the criminal for the wrong done — an eye for an eye. Controversial as it doesn't put right the wrong.
Deterrence: making the punishment severe enough that others won't commit the same crime.
Reformation: changing the criminal's behaviour so they don't reoffend. Many religious people favour this approach.
Protection: keeping dangerous criminals away from society (e.g. prison).
Reparation: making the criminal pay back or make amends to the victim or society (e.g. community service).
Capital Punishment

The Death Penalty

Capital punishment has been abolished in most of Europe and South America. Where used, it's for very serious crimes.
Arguments for: stronger deterrent than prison; ensures murderers can't reoffend; proportional punishment for very serious crimes.
Arguments against: killing as punishment is as bad as murder; risk of executing innocent people; doesn't deter crime; violates the sanctity of life.
Christianity: most oppose it as it violates the sanctity of life, prevents repentance and removes any chance of reformation.
Islam: Shari'ah law allows the death penalty for certain crimes (murder, adultery in some interpretations), but requires high standards of proof. Forgiveness from the victim's family is always preferred.
F
Thematic Study · Theme F
Religion, Human Rights & Social Justice
Equality, prejudice, wealth, poverty and freedom of belief
Equality

Attitudes to Equality

Prejudice: judging without knowledge. Discrimination: treating people unjustly because of prejudice.
Types include: sexism, racism, homophobia, ageism, and disability discrimination.
The Equality Act 2010 makes discrimination on 'protected characteristics' illegal in the UK.
Christianity: "There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus" (Galatians 3:28).
Islam: "Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you" (Qur'an 49:13). All Muslims are equal before Allah.
Human Rights

Human Rights

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) set out minimum rights for every person: right to life, freedom from slavery, freedom of opinion and expression, etc.
The European Convention on Human Rights (1953) made these legally enforceable in European courts.
All three Abrahamic religions support human rights, as all humans are created in God's image and are of equal value.
Martin Luther King Jr. used Christian teaching to fight racial discrimination in America. Desmond Tutu used his faith to oppose apartheid in South Africa.
Malcolm X used Islamic teaching to fight for Black American rights, arguing all people are brothers and sisters before Allah.
Belief

Freedom of Belief

Freedom of religion and belief is a legal right in the UK — people can follow any or no religion.
People are protected from discrimination because of their beliefs.
However, religious exemptions can sometimes conflict with other rights — e.g. a registrar refusing to conduct same-sex civil partnerships on religious grounds.
Christianity: Jesus taught "love your neighbour" — some Christians believe this means accepting people of all faiths without forcing their own beliefs on others.
Islam: "There is no compulsion in religion" (Qur'an 2:256) — Islam does not require forced conversion.
Justice

Social Justice

Social justice means treating all groups fairly, eliminating discrimination, and redistributing wealth so everyone can afford to live.
Causes of poverty in the UK: low wages, high costs (housing, childcare), lack of skills, unemployment.
The National Living Wage exists but many argue it's still not enough for a comfortable life.
All three Abrahamic religions emphasise caring for the poor and vulnerable as a key religious duty.
Wealth

Wealth & Poverty

Christianity: "You cannot serve both God and money" (Matthew 6:24). Wealth should be earned morally and used to help others. The Church of England runs financial literacy workshops to combat exploitative lenders.
Islam: Zakah (2.5% of savings) is obligatory. Sadaqah (voluntary charity) is encouraged. Riba (interest) is forbidden (Qur'an 2:275). Islamic banking avoids interest to prevent exploitation.
Islamic finance: money shouldn't be used to make more money passively — this ensures wealth is more fairly distributed.
Both religions condemn profiting from harmful industries (alcohol, weapons, exploitation).
G
Thematic Study · Theme G — Textual
St Mark's Gospel: The Life of Jesus
Ministry, miracles, passion and resurrection
Beginning

The Start of Jesus's Ministry (1:4–11)

Mark's Gospel begins with John the Baptist, not Jesus's birth. John quotes the Old Testament: he is the messenger who will "prepare the way for the Lord" (Mark 1:3).
John baptised people in the River Jordan by total immersion as a sign of repentance.
At Jesus's baptism, the Holy Spirit descended "like a dove" and God's voice said "You are my Son, whom I love" (Mark 1:11). This affirms Jesus as the Messiah.
Jesus then spent 40 days in the desert, tempted by Satan, before beginning his ministry in Galilee.
Miracles

Jesus's Miracles — The Paralysed Man (2:1–12)

Four men lowered a paralysed man through the roof of a house to reach Jesus in a crowd.
Jesus first forgave the man's sins — shocking the religious authorities: "Who can forgive sins but God alone?" (Mark 2:7).
Jesus then healed the man physically, demonstrating his authority as the Son of Man to forgive sins.
Key message: Jesus has divine authority. Forgiveness of sins is more important than physical healing.
"But I want you to know that the Son of Man has authority on earth to forgive sins."
Mark 2:10 NIV
Miracles

Feeding the Five Thousand (6:30–44)

With only five loaves and two fish, Jesus fed a crowd of at least 5000 people, with 12 baskets of leftovers.
This reminded Jewish listeners of God feeding the Israelites with manna in the wilderness (Exodus).
For Christians today, it foreshadows the Last Supper — Jesus breaks bread as he would at the Eucharist.
Shows Jesus's compassion for people's physical and spiritual needs.
"Taking the five loaves and the two fish and looking up to heaven, he gave thanks and broke the loaves."
Mark 6:41 NIV
Turning Point

Peter's Declaration & The Passion Predictions (8:27–33)

Near Caesarea Philippi, Jesus asked "Who do people say I am?" — the disciples reported various opinions.
Peter declared "You are the Messiah" (Mark 8:29) — a turning point in the Gospel.
Jesus then began his journey towards Jerusalem and made three passion predictions — foretelling his suffering, death and resurrection.
The third prediction (Mark 10:33–34) was the most detailed: "They will condemn him to death... mock him... flog him and kill him. Three days later he will rise."
Final Days

The Last Supper & Trial (14–15)

The Last Supper was a Passover meal. Jesus broke bread saying "This is my body" and passed wine saying "This is my blood of the covenant" (Mark 14:22–24).
Jesus predicted one of the disciples (Judas) would betray him, and that Peter would deny him three times.
Gethsemane: Jesus prayed for God to "take this cup from me, yet not what I will, but what you will" (Mark 14:36) — showing both human anguish and submission to God.
Jesus was arrested, tried by the Sanhedrin for blasphemy, then handed to Pilate who had him crucified after the crowd chose Barabbas instead.
Crucifixion: at Golgotha, Jesus died with the words "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Mark 15:34). The Temple curtain tore and a centurion declared "Surely this man was the Son of God!" (Mark 15:39).

Thematic Study · Theme H — Textual
St Mark's Gospel: Spiritual Truths
Kingdom of God, 1st century society, faith and discipleship
Kingdom of God

The Kingdom of God

The kingdom of God is the time and place where God rules. Jesus's opening message was: "The time has come. The kingdom of God has come near. Repent and believe the good news!" (Mark 1:15).
The kingdom can refer to a present reality in people's hearts, a community of believers, or a future physical kingdom when Jesus returns in the Second Coming.
Parable of the Mustard Seed (4:30–32): the kingdom of God starts tiny but grows huge — like the Church growing from a few disciples to millions of Christians worldwide.
Welcoming children (10:13–16): the kingdom belongs to those who receive it with childlike openness and trust.
Greatest Commandments: love God and love your neighbour — these summarise the path to the kingdom.
"Your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven."
Matthew 6:10 NIV
Outcasts

People Disregarded by Society

Many people were outcasts in 1st century Jewish society — excluded from worship and social life.

Sinners: those who broke Jewish law were cut off from God's people. Jesus ate with them (Mark 2:15), scandalising the religious leaders.
The sick and diseased: considered unclean — touching them made you unclean too. Jesus healed them without hesitation.
Gentiles (non-Jews): considered outside God's people. The Syrophoenician woman (7:24–30) — Jesus healed her daughter after her faith and humility moved him.
The poor: couldn't afford temple sacrifices, so remained sinful. Jesus condemned the exploitation of the poor (Mark 12:40).
Jesus consistently reached out to outcasts — showing that the kingdom of God is for everyone.
Faith

Faith in Mark's Gospel

The bleeding woman (5:25–34): had suffered for 12 years. She touched Jesus's cloak in faith and was healed. Jesus said "your faith has healed you" (Mark 5:34).
Faith in Mark means trusting God — and acting on that trust. The woman's faith was demonstrated by her action of seeking Jesus out.
Jairus's daughter (5:21–43): Jairus begged Jesus to heal his dying daughter. Despite being told she had died, Jesus said "Don't be afraid; just believe" (Mark 5:36) and raised her to life.
Lack of faith limited Jesus's miracles — in Nazareth, "He could not do any miracles there... because of their lack of faith" (Mark 6:5–6).
"Daughter, your faith has healed you. Go in peace and be freed from your suffering."
Mark 5:34 NIV
Discipleship

Faith and Discipleship

Jesus chose twelve disciples — symbolising the twelve tribes of Israel and signalling a new chosen people of God.
The first disciples (Simon, Andrew, James, John) immediately left their fishing nets and followed Jesus — showing complete commitment.
The disciples were sent out in pairs to preach, heal and drive out demons. They took no food, money or luggage — relying on others' hospitality.
Peter's denial (14:66–72): despite swearing he'd never desert Jesus, Peter denied knowing him three times after the arrest — showing that even the strongest faith can fail under pressure.
Modern discipleship: following Jesus's vocation (e.g. becoming a priest), living out his teachings through kindness and service, and accepting Jesus's message with childlike faith.

Exam Guidance
Do Well in Your Exam
Structure, question types and SPaG advice

Exam Structure

  • Two papers, each 1 hour 45 minutes, each worth 50% of marks.
  • Paper 1: Beliefs, Teachings and Practices for two religions (50 mins each). 10-part questions per religion.
  • Paper 2: Four thematic studies (25 mins each). 5-part questions per theme.
  • If studying St Mark's Gospel (Themes G & H), you answer one religion in Paper 1 + one other, then Themes G & H plus 2 others in Paper 2.

Question Types

  • 1 mark: Multiple choice — one correct answer.
  • 2 mark: Give two points/beliefs/examples. Be concise.
  • 4 mark: Explain how a belief influences believers. Make 2 developed points.
  • 5 mark: Explain two things — include a sacred text reference or quotation.
  • 12 mark: Extended writing — present both sides and reach a conclusion. SPaG marks available.
📝 5-Mark Question Tip

You must refer to a sacred text or teaching. One reference is enough — state which book (e.g. "In Genesis…") or quote and name the source. Paraphrasing counts as a reference.

📝 12-Mark Question Tip

Present both sides of an argument — agree AND disagree. Use technical religious terms correctly. Leave 5 minutes at the end to check spelling, punctuation and grammar.

SPaG — Spelling, Punctuation & Grammar

SPaG marks are only available on the 12-mark questions. Examiners look at overall quality of writing AND use of technical religious vocabulary (e.g. Eucharist, Atonement, Tawhid, Rak'ah).

Watch out for: homophones ('their/there/they're', 'affect/effect'), writing in full words (no text speak), checking for missing words, and beginning sentences with capital letters. Reserve your final check time for these questions only — they're the ones that count.
